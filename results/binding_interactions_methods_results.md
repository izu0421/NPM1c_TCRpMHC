# TCR:pMHC binding-interface analysis

## Methods

Structural models of each TCR:peptide–MHC complex were generated with TCRdock (AlphaFold-based) and the predicted coordinates parsed directly from the output PDB files. For every model, atoms were assigned to one of four chain segments (MHC, peptide, TCRα, TCRβ) using the known residue-count boundaries of each chain. Inter-chain contacts between the TCR (α and β chains combined) and either the peptide or the MHC were then enumerated from all heavy-atom pairs. Interactions were classified geometrically, using distance cut-offs applied to the relevant atom types: salt bridges between oppositely charged side-chain atoms (Arg/Lys/His N atoms and Asp/Glu O atoms) within 4.0 Å; hydrogen bonds between nitrogen or oxygen donor/acceptor atoms within 3.5 Å (hydrogen atoms were not modelled, so N/O proximity was used as a proxy); disulfide/covalent bonds between cysteine Sγ atoms within 2.5 Å; and hydrophobic van der Waals contacts between carbon atoms of nonpolar residues (Ala, Val, Leu, Ile, Met, Phe, Trp, Pro, Cys) within 4.0 Å, retaining only the closest carbon–carbon contact per residue pair. To describe the TCR footprint on the peptide, any TCR residue with a heavy atom within 4.5 Å of a peptide atom was defined as a contact residue. Interfaces were visualised in three dimensions with py3Dmol, showing the peptide and contacting TCR residues as sticks (TCRα, blue; TCRβ, green; peptide, orange) with dashed lines coloured by interaction type; the three binder panels shared a linked camera to allow direct comparison. Analyses were restricted to the three confirmed binders (lowest pMHC–TCR PAE), and the TCR–peptide and TCR–MHC interfaces were tabulated separately.

## Results

The three confirmed binders — TCR5 and TCR29 recognising the HLA-A*02:01–restricted peptide AIQDLCLAV, and TCR24 recognising CLAVEEVSL — showed low predicted pMHC–TCR PAE values (5.46, 5.55 and 6.78, respectively), consistent with confident interface predictions. Dissecting the interfaces by target revealed that the peptide was engaged almost exclusively through hydrogen bonding: each binder formed exactly three TCR–peptide hydrogen bonds, with TCR29 and TCR24 contributing one additional hydrophobic contact each and no salt bridges or disulfides to the peptide. The MHC surface accounted for the majority of the interface in every case. TCR5 made the most extensive MHC contact (10 hydrogen bonds and no other bond types), whereas TCR29 (7 hydrogen bonds plus 1 salt bridge) and TCR24 (2 hydrogen bonds, 1 salt bridge and 1 hydrophobic contact) engaged the MHC more modestly, with TCR24 showing the smallest overall MHC footprint.

Mapping the TCR footprint on the peptide identified 7–10 contacting TCR residues per binder (heavy atom ≤ 4.5 Å): 10 residues for TCR5, 7 for TCR29 and 8 for TCR24. In each complex two of these contact residues made an explicit named bond, while the remainder formed close van der Waals contacts. Both chains contributed to the peptide interface: TCR5 contacted the peptide through four TCRα residues (Q615, S616, N676, G678, F680) and four TCRβ residues (Y945, E990, T991, G993, S994), with the α-chain Q615 and β-chain E990 forming the named hydrogen bonds; TCR29 used TCRα residues R679, A680, N681, S682 together with TCRβ W993, G996 and F998 (bonds via αS682 and βF998); and TCR24 combined TCRα G613, Q615 and T676 with TCRβ R947, P987, G988, L990 and S991 (bonds via αQ615 and βL990). Together, these analyses indicate that peptide specificity in all three binders is mediated by a small, conserved set of hydrogen bonds distributed across both TCR chains, superimposed on a larger, more variable hydrogen-bond-dominated interface with the MHC.
